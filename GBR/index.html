Great Barrier Reef Bleaching Occurrences, 1980-2002

Authored by: Schroeder1303



The Great Barrier Reef is the largest and most well-known coral reef system in the world. Covering an area of more than 344,000 square kilometers (132,800 miles), it is listed as a UNESCO World Heritage site.


Marine Protected Areas (MPAs) are areas that are allocated to achieve long-term conservation goals. There are several different types - ranging from no access (typically research only) to multiple use. Read more about the different definitions and use types.


Coral bleaching occurs when stress factors (either from high water temperatures, overexposure to sunlight, pollution or exposure to air from extremely low tides) causes coral to expel the algae that lives on them, causing the coral to turn white. The coral is not dead, but the algae is their primary food source thus making them susceptible to disease and death. Fortunately once coral has been bleached, it cannot be bleached again and if the stressors do not remain for an extended period of time, the coral can recover. Read more about coral bleaching.
